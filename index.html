About this Study

Is the compliance of mobile applications (apps) with their privacy policy getting better or worse over time? In this paper, we answer this question by analyzing the historical versions of 268 popular Android applications comprising 5,240 app releases between 2008 to 2016. We detect inconsistencies in apps' behaviour and stated use of data collection to reveal compliance issues. Essentially, we utilize machine learning techniques to classify the privacy policy text to identify the purported practices that collect and/or share personal data (e.g., phone number). We then uncover the actual data leaks of an app through static analysis (examining the app code) and dynamic analysis (inspecting the network traffic generated by the app). Our results show a steady annual increase in the number of practices performed by the app that are contrary to the practices disclosed in the privacy policies. This is particularly troubling since privacy policy is the primary tool for describing the app's privacy practices. We find that newer versions of the apps are likely to be more non-compliant than the preceding versions. The discrepancies between the purported and actual data practices exposed that privacy policies are incoherent with the apps' behavior, thus defying the 'notice and choice' principle when users install the apps.
